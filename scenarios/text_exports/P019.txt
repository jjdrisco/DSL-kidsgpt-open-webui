---

### 1. Civic Engagement & Personal Growth  

**Argument For:**  
- Supporters say required community service helps students build empathy, responsibility, and real-world skills.  
- It can expose students to different communities and social issues they might not otherwise encounter.  
- Some research suggests teens who volunteer are more likely to stay engaged in civic life as adults.

**Argument Against:**  
- Critics argue that forced volunteering may reduce genuine motivation. If students are required to do it, it may feel like a chore instead of meaningful service.  
- Some studies suggest mandatory service doesn’t always lead to long-term civic engagement — voluntary service tends to have stronger positive effects.

**Possible Sources to Look Up:**  
- Research from the *Corporation for National and Community Service (AmeriCorps)*  
- Studies published in journals like *Youth & Society*  
- Articles from Pew Research Center on teen civic engagement  

---

### 2. Fairness & Access  

**Argument For:**  
- Schools can level the playing field by helping all students access service opportunities (transportation, structured programs).  
- A requirement ensures every graduate has some real-world experience beyond academics.

**Argument Against:**  
- Not all students have equal free time. Some have jobs, family responsibilities, or transportation barriers.  
- A requirement might unfairly burden low-income students or students who already contribute to their families in unpaid ways.

**Possible Sources to Look Up:**  
- Education policy discussions from *Brookings Institution*  
- National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) data on student work and responsibilities  
- Education Week articles on equity in graduation requirements  

---

### 3. Educational Value vs. Academic Focus  

**Argument For:**  
- Community service can connect classroom learning to real-world problems (service-learning models).  
- It may help develop soft skills like teamwork, communication, and leadership.

**Argument Against:**  
- Schools already have packed academic requirements. Adding mandatory service could shift focus away from core subjects.  
- Some argue community involvement should be encouraged but not tied to graduation requirements.

**Possible Sources to Look Up:**  
- National Youth Leadership Council (research on service-learning)  
- RAND Corporation education policy reports  
- State Department of Education websites (for examples of states that require service hours)
